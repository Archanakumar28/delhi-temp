Tracing the Temporary

A Digital Humanities Archive of Delhi’s Ephemeral Architecture

Delhi is often understood through what endures—its monuments, ruins, and institutional spaces. Yet alongside this permanence exists another city: one that is constantly built, transformed, and dismantled.

Across neighborhoods and streets, temporary structures emerge—festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and pop-up markets. They reshape movement, redefine public space, and gather communities, only to disappear shortly after.

These structures do not merely occupy space—they perform it.

Built to Transform

Temporary architecture in Delhi does not simply occupy space—it transforms it. An empty ground becomes a site of ritual, a street turns into a procession route, and a public intersection evolves into a space of gathering and spectacle. These structures interrupt the everyday rhythm of the city, reconfiguring movement, visibility, and interaction. In doing so, they reveal that urban space is not fixed, but constantly produced through cultural activity and collective presence.

Designed to Communicate

Temporary architecture in Delhi is never neutral—it communicates. Through color, scale, material, and form, these structures convey meanings that are immediately legible within their cultural contexts. A religious pandal may draw on mythological imagery to evoke devotion and shared belief, a wedding tent may signal aspiration and social identity through its opulence and illumination, while a protest stage asserts urgency and visibility through its placement and design. Every element is intentional: fabric, lighting, ornamentation, and spatial arrangement together function as a visual language. In this way, temporary architecture becomes a medium of communication, transforming built space into a site where cultural narratives, identities, and emotions are actively expressed and interpreted.

Meant to Disappear

Temporary architecture is defined as much by its disappearance as by its presence. Built with the knowledge that it will be dismantled, it exists within a limited yet intense temporal frame, marking specific moments of ritual, celebration, or political expression. Its removal is not merely practical but meaningful, signaling the closure of an event and the transition of space back to its everyday state. Yet even after these structures vanish, they leave behind traces—memories, altered spatial experiences, and visual records. This ephemerality challenges conventional ideas of heritage and permanence, suggesting that cultural significance does not depend on durability but can reside in fleeting, repeated, and collectively experienced moments that continue to shape how the city is remembered.

“The present contains nothing more than the past, and what is found in the effect was already in the cause.”
