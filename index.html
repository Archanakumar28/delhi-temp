Tracing the Temporary

A Digital Humanities Archive of Delhi’s Ephemeral Spaces

Delhi is often remembered through what lasts-its forts, monuments, and carefully preserved past. But there’s another city that rarely makes it into history books. A city of pandals that rise overnight, wedding tents that glow for a single evening, protest stages that transform streets into statements, and markets that appear and disappear like they were never there.

Across neighborhoods, temporary architectures emerge; festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and seasonal markets. They reorganize movement, gather communities, and reimagine public space.

These structures activate the city, turning ordinary spaces into sites of ritual, celebration, and expression.

These structures do not merely occupy space—they perform it.
This project is about "that Delhi".

Designed to Communicate

Beneath the fading light of the city, the photograph of Rain Basera begins to speak—not in words, but in quiet, persistent truths. This is not just a shelter; it is a pause in the relentless movement of urban life. Here, stories gather. A worn blanket folded in the corner, a pair of sandals left by the doorway, the faint glow of a shared bulb, each detail holds a fragment of someone’s journey. Designed to communicate, this space reveals what often goes unseen. It tells of migration and survival, of nights spent negotiating uncertainty, and of mornings that begin without guarantees. Yet, within these fragile walls, there is also a quiet resilience. People arrive as strangers, but for a few hours, they share a common ground; rest, warmth, and the simple dignity of being sheltered. The image does not demand attention; it invites reflection. It asks the viewer to look closer, to listen beyond the visible. In doing so, Rain Basera becomes more than a subject, it becomes a storyteller, carrying voices that are often left unheard in the larger narrative of the city.

Meant to Disappear

For a few brief days, the Durga Puja pandal rises like a world unto itself, bright, intricate, impossible to ignore. In the photograph, its details seem permanent: the carefully crafted arches, the textured walls, the play of light and color that transforms ordinary space into something sacred. People enter, pause, and look up in quiet awe, as if this place has always existed and always will. But it is meant to disappear. Built with devotion and imagination, the pandal carries within it the knowledge of its own end. The same hands that shaped it understand that soon it will be dismantled, its materials taken apart, its presence reduced to memory. What remains then is not the structure, but the experience, the sound of drums echoing at dusk, the scent of incense lingering in the air, the fleeting feeling of togetherness. In this way, the pandal tells a different kind of story. It reminds us that not all spaces are meant to last. Some are created precisely for their impermanence, for the intensity of a moment that cannot be held onto. And perhaps that is why they matter more because they ask to be felt fully, before they quietly vanish.

Rooted in Memory

On a stretch of campus where footsteps never quite fade, the Wall of Democracy stands layered with voices, some bold, some fading, some written in haste before they could be erased. The photograph captures more than paint on concrete; it holds echoes. Every slogan, every poster half-torn, every overlapping message carries a memory of a moment when someone felt the need to speak, to resist, to belong. Rooted in memory, this wall is never still. What you see today is only a fragment of what has been yesterday’s demands painted over by today’s urgency, last semester’s protests dissolving into new calls for change. And yet, nothing truly disappears. Beneath each fresh layer lies a history of dissent and dialogue, quietly persisting. Students pass by, sometimes pausing, sometimes adding their own words to the surface. In those acts, the wall becomes more than an object; it becomes a living archive. It remembers voices even when they are gone, holds space for those yet to speak, and preserves the rhythm of a campus constantly negotiating its ideas of justice, identity, and belonging.
