About the Project

Tracing the Temporary

Introduction

Ephemeral Delhi is a digital humanities project that turns attention toward a dimension of the city that is often overlooked: the temporary. While Delhi is commonly understood through its monuments, ruins, and enduring architectural forms, it is equally shaped by structures that exist only for a moment. These transient architectures, including festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and informal markets, appear, transform space, and disappear, leaving behind traces not in material permanence but in memory, experience, and cultural practice. This project approaches these fleeting structures not as peripheral or insignificant, but as central to understanding how the city is continuously produced. Temporary architecture in Delhi does more than occupy space; it activates it. Streets become sites of procession, open grounds turn into ritual landscapes, and public spaces are reconfigured through collective participation. In these moments, the city is not static; it is performed, negotiated, and reimagined. Ephemeral Delhi seeks to document, archive, and interpret these transient spatial forms. By combining visual documentation, mapping, and analytical frameworks, the project builds a record of structures that would otherwise vanish without a trace. It treats these architectures as cultural texts, that is, sites where identity, aspiration, devotion, and power are expressed through material, design, and spatial arrangement.

Core Idea

The central premise of Ephemeral Delhi is that temporary architecture is not marginal to the life of the city, but fundamental to how urban space is produced and experienced. These short-lived structures, whether festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, or informal markets, emerge within specific cultural, social, and political contexts. In doing so, they transform ordinary spaces into sites of heightened activity and meaning. Defined by their intentional impermanence, these architectures exist within limited temporal frames, yet they actively reorganize spatial relations and collective experience. They guide movement, create gathering points, and reshape how space is perceived and used. Rather than being merely functional, their materials, designs, and forms communicate identity, aspiration, and power, turning built space into a medium of expression. At the same time, their disappearance does not signal their end. These structures persist through memory, repetition, and documentation, leaving lasting impressions on how the city is understood and remembered. By focusing on these transient forms, Ephemeral Delhi challenges conventional ideas of permanence and proposes a view of the city as dynamic, performative, and continuously in the making.

Research Questions

  • How does temporary architecture reflect cultural and political life?
  • What meanings are embedded in design and materials?
  • How do these structures reshape public space?
  • What remains after they disappear?

What We Study

Across Delhi, temporary architecture takes on multiple forms, each shaped by its purpose and context.

Religious Structures

Religious structures often merge devotion with spectacle, creating immersive environments in which ritual and storytelling unfold simultaneously. These spaces are designed for collective experience, drawing people in through scale, colour, ornamentation, and symbolic imagery that transforms belief into spatial form.

Social Spaces

Social structures, particularly those built for weddings and community gatherings, turn space into a reflection of identity and aspiration. They are carefully curated environments where design operates as a language of status, celebration, and belonging, shaping how individuals and communities present themselves in public.

Political Structures

Political structures function differently. Protest stages and rally sites are not only platforms but assertions of presence. They occupy space strategically, making visibility, voice, and gathering central to their function. In these moments, architecture becomes inseparable from expressions of power and resistance.

Commercial Spaces

Commercial setups, including markets, stalls, and pop-up vendors, follow the rhythms of the city. They emerge in response to demand and opportunity, adapting fluidly to available space. These structures reveal the everyday negotiations of urban life and the informal systems that sustain it.

Together, these varied forms demonstrate that temporary architecture is not marginal, but deeply embedded in the city’s daily and seasonal cycles, continuously shaping how urban space is produced and experienced.

Theoretical Framework

The theoretical foundation of Ephemeral Delhi is grounded in spatial theory, performance studies, and visual culture, with a focus on how space is socially produced, enacted, and remembered. Rather than treating architecture as a static object, this project understands it as a process, shaped by cultural practices, collective behaviour, and temporal conditions. A key influence is Henri Lefebvre’s concept of the production of space, which argues that space is not merely a physical container but is actively produced through social relations, practices, and representations (Lefebvre). In this framework, temporary architectures in Delhi can be understood as moments where space is visibly produced in real time. Festival structures, protest stages, and seasonal markets do not simply occupy space; they reorganize it through collective use, thereby revealing the socially constructed nature of the urban environment. This perspective is further supported by performance theory, particularly the work of Richard Schechner, who describes performance as “restored behaviour,” where cultural actions are repeated, rehearsed, and re-enacted across time (Schechner). Temporary architecture functions similarly, as it is repeatedly constructed and dismantled in cyclical patterns tied to ritual, celebration, and political expression. Each iteration is both new and familiar, reinforcing cultural memory through embodied spatial practice. The project also draws on visual culture studies, especially Nicholas Mirzoeff’s notion of the “right to look,” which frames visuality as a contested space of power, representation, and resistance (Mirzoeff). Temporary structures in Delhi operate within this visual field, often designed to be seen, to assert presence, or to communicate identity. Whether through religious iconography, political messaging, or celebratory display, these architectures actively participate in the production of visual meaning in public space. Together, these theoretical frameworks position temporary architecture as a site where space, performance, and visuality intersect. They allow Ephemeral Delhi to read the city not as a fixed architectural landscape, but as an evolving field of cultural expression, continuously produced through transient, embodied, and visual practices.

Digital Humanities Approach

Using mapping, photography, and metadata, the project builds a digital archive of structures that would otherwise leave no record.

Why It Matters

Most archives preserve what remains. This project focuses on what disappears. It expands the idea of heritage by showing how memory persists beyond physical form.

Thesis Statement

Temporary architecture in Delhi functions as a performative cultural text that transforms public space, produces collective memory, and reflects shifting identities.
