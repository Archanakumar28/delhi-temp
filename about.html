About the Project

Ephemeral Delhi: A Digital Humanities Archive of Temporary Architecture

Introduction

Delhi is often understood through what endures—its monuments, ruins, and institutional architecture. Yet alongside this permanence exists another city: one that is constantly built, transformed, and dismantled. This project turns attention to that overlooked layer—temporary structures that shape urban experience in powerful but short-lived ways.

Ephemeral Delhi is a digital humanities project that documents and analyzes these structures as cultural texts. Rather than treating them as functional or decorative, the project interprets them as meaningful spatial expressions that reflect the city’s cultural, religious, social, and political life.

Core Idea

Temporary architecture in Delhi consists of structures that appear rapidly, transform public space, and disappear shortly after. These include festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and seasonal marketplaces.

Despite their short lifespan, these spaces are highly symbolic and culturally dense. They embody collective identity, social aspiration, political presence, and ritual performance. The project argues that these structures should be read not as temporary utilities, but as active cultural expressions embedded in the life of the city.

Research Questions

  • How does temporary architecture reflect Delhi’s cultural, religious, and political life?
  • What meanings are embedded in design, material, and visual form?
  • How do these structures reshape public space and social interaction?
  • What happens to cultural memory once these structures disappear?

What We Study

Religious Structures

Festival pandals, mandaps, and ritual installations that merge devotion with spectacle. These spaces often incorporate mythological narratives, contemporary themes, and elaborate visual design.

Social & Family Spaces

Wedding tents, community feasts, and ceremonial spaces that express identity, class, and aspiration through decoration, lighting, and scale.

Political & Protest Architecture

Temporary stages, barricades, and protest sites that transform public space into arenas of visibility, resistance, and power.

Commercial Pop-Up Spaces

Seasonal markets and temporary stalls that reflect patterns of consumption, mobility, and informal economies within the city.

Theoretical Framework

This project is grounded in key ideas from urban and cultural theory. It draws on the concept of the production of space, which understands space as something socially created rather than fixed. It also engages with performance theory, viewing architecture as an event that unfolds over time, and memory studies, which examine how experiences persist beyond physical form.

Together, these perspectives allow the project to interpret temporary architecture as a dynamic cultural process rather than static structure.

Digital Humanities Approach

Because these structures are ephemeral, digital tools become essential for preservation. The project uses mapping, visual documentation, and metadata organization to create an interactive archive of spaces that would otherwise leave no record.

  • Interactive spatial mapping of sites
  • Photographic documentation across time
  • Metadata tagging (occasion, material, duration, context)
  • Visual and textual analysis

This approach transforms fleeting experiences into structured, accessible knowledge.

Methodology

The project follows a phased methodology combining fieldwork and digital analysis. Selected sites across Delhi are documented through photography and observation. Data is organized into a structured format and visualized through an interactive map. This is followed by cultural analysis that connects field observations with theoretical frameworks.

The method remains flexible, allowing the project to expand across time, space, and themes.

Why It Matters

Most archives preserve what remains. This project focuses on what disappears.

Temporary architecture reveals that urban space is not fixed—it is continuously produced through ritual, celebration, and political action. By documenting these transformations, the project expands the idea of heritage beyond permanence and highlights the role of impermanence in shaping cultural memory.

Project Vision

Ephemeral Delhi is designed as an evolving archive. It can expand across years, locations, and categories, allowing for longitudinal and comparative study.

Future directions include year-wise comparisons, design evolution, cross-city studies, and enhanced digital visualization.

Thesis Statement

Temporary architecture in Delhi functions as a performative cultural text that transforms public space, produces collective memory, and reflects shifting social and political identities.
