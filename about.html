About the Project

A Digital Humanities Archive of Temporary Architecture

Introduction

Delhi is often understood through what endures—its monuments, ruins, and institutional architecture. Yet alongside this permanence exists another city: one that is constantly built, transformed, and dismantled. This project turns attention to that overlooked layer—temporary structures that shape urban experience in powerful but short-lived ways.

Ephemeral Delhi documents these structures as cultural texts, interpreting them not as temporary utilities but as meaningful spatial expressions that reflect the city’s social, religious, and political life.

Core Idea

Temporary architecture in Delhi consists of structures that appear rapidly, transform public space, and disappear shortly after. These include festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and seasonal marketplaces.

Despite their short lifespan, these spaces are highly symbolic. They communicate identity, aspiration, devotion, and power, revealing that urban space is not fixed but continuously produced through cultural activity.

Research Questions

  • How does temporary architecture reflect cultural and political life?
  • What meanings are embedded in design and materials?
  • How do these structures reshape public space?
  • What remains after they disappear?

What We Study

Religious Structures

Festival spaces that merge devotion with spectacle and storytelling.

Social Spaces

Wedding and community structures that express identity and aspiration.

Political Structures

Protest and rally spaces that shape visibility and power.

Commercial Spaces

Markets and stalls reflecting urban rhythms and informal economies.

Theoretical Framework

The project draws on ideas of spatial production, performance, and memory. It understands space as socially created and architecture as an event rather than a static object.

Digital Humanities Approach

Using mapping, photography, and metadata, the project builds a digital archive of structures that would otherwise leave no record.

Why It Matters

Most archives preserve what remains. This project focuses on what disappears. It expands the idea of heritage by showing how memory persists beyond physical form.

Thesis Statement

Temporary architecture in Delhi functions as a performative cultural text that transforms public space, produces collective memory, and reflects shifting identities.
