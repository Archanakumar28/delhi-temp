About the Project

Tracing the Temporary

Introduction

Ephemeral Delhi is a digital humanities project that turns attention toward a dimension of the city that is often overlooked: the temporary. While Delhi is commonly understood through its monuments, ruins, and enduring architectural forms, it is equally shaped by structures that exist only for a moment. These transient architectures, including festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, and informal markets, appear, transform space, and disappear, leaving behind traces not in material permanence but in memory, experience, and cultural practice. This project approaches these fleeting structures not as peripheral or insignificant, but as central to understanding how the city is continuously produced. Temporary architecture in Delhi does more than occupy space; it activates it. Streets become sites of procession, open grounds turn into ritual landscapes, and public spaces are reconfigured through collective participation. In these moments, the city is not static; it is performed, negotiated, and reimagined. Ephemeral Delhi seeks to document, archive, and interpret these transient spatial forms. By combining visual documentation, mapping, and analytical frameworks, the project builds a record of structures that would otherwise vanish without a trace. It treats these architectures as cultural texts, that is, sites where identity, aspiration, devotion, and power are expressed through material, design, and spatial arrangement.

Core Idea

The central premise of Ephemeral Delhi is that temporary architecture is not marginal to the life of the city, but fundamental to how urban space is produced and experienced. These short-lived structures, whether festival pandals, wedding tents, protest stages, or informal markets, emerge within specific cultural, social, and political contexts. In doing so, they transform ordinary spaces into sites of heightened activity and meaning. Defined by their intentional impermanence, these architectures exist within limited temporal frames, yet they actively reorganize spatial relations and collective experience. They guide movement, create gathering points, and reshape how space is perceived and used. Rather than being merely functional, their materials, designs, and forms communicate identity, aspiration, and power, turning built space into a medium of expression. At the same time, their disappearance does not signal their end. These structures persist through memory, repetition, and documentation, leaving lasting impressions on how the city is understood and remembered. By focusing on these transient forms, Ephemeral Delhi challenges conventional ideas of permanence and proposes a view of the city as dynamic, performative, and continuously in the making.

Research Questions

  • How does temporary architecture reflect cultural and political life?
  • What meanings are embedded in design and materials?
  • How do these structures reshape public space?
  • What remains after they disappear?

What We Study

Religious Structures

Festival spaces that merge devotion with spectacle and storytelling.

Social Spaces

Wedding and community structures that express identity and aspiration.

Political Structures

Protest and rally spaces that shape visibility and power.

Commercial Spaces

Markets and stalls reflecting urban rhythms and informal economies.

Theoretical Framework

The project draws on ideas of spatial production, performance, and memory. It understands space as socially created and architecture as an event rather than a static object.

Digital Humanities Approach

Using mapping, photography, and metadata, the project builds a digital archive of structures that would otherwise leave no record.

Why It Matters

Most archives preserve what remains. This project focuses on what disappears. It expands the idea of heritage by showing how memory persists beyond physical form.

Thesis Statement

Temporary architecture in Delhi functions as a performative cultural text that transforms public space, produces collective memory, and reflects shifting identities.
